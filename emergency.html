First Aid Guides

Step-by-step visual guides. Follow each step carefully in an emergency.

Stop the Bleeding — Tourniquet Guide

Severe bleeding from a limb can be life-threatening. Act immediately.

URGENT
1

Apply Pressure

Press firmly on the wound with a clean cloth. Do NOT remove the cloth. Keep pressing hard.

2

Tie the Tourniquet

Place the tourniquet 2–3 inches above the wound. Pull the tail tight — as tight as possible.

3

Twist to Stop Bleed

Twist the windlass rod until the bleeding stops completely. Secure it in the triangle clasp.

4

Note the Time

Write the exact time you applied the tourniquet on the person's skin or tourniquet. Get to a hospital IMMEDIATELY.

Important: Never remove a tourniquet once applied. Do NOT leave on for more than 2 hours. Call 999 immediately.

CPR — Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation

Use when someone is unconscious and not breathing normally.

LIFE-SAVING
1

Check & Call

Tap their shoulders and shout "Are you OK?" If no response, call 999 immediately. Send someone for help.

2

30 Chest Compressions

Place hands on centre of chest. Push down hard and fast — 2 inches deep. Do 30 compressions at 100–120 per minute.

3

2 Rescue Breaths

Tilt head back, lift chin. Pinch nose closed. Give 2 slow breaths — each about 1 second long. Watch chest rise.

4

Repeat Until Help Arrives

Continue 30 compressions then 2 breaths. Keep going until the person starts breathing or emergency services arrive.

Tip: Push to the beat of "Stayin' Alive" by Bee Gees — that's exactly 100 beats per minute.

Choking — Heimlich Manoeuvre

When someone cannot breathe, cough, or speak due to something stuck in their throat.

URGENT
1

Identify Choking

Look for: hands at throat, cannot speak or cry, blue lips, silent cough. Ask "Are you choking?" — if yes, act now.

2

5 Back Blows

Lean them forward. Give 5 firm blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand. Check after each.

3

5 Abdominal Thrusts

Stand behind them. Make a fist above navel. Pull sharply inward and upward 5 times. Alternate with back blows.

4

Call 999

If they lose consciousness, lower them gently to the ground and begin CPR. Call 999 immediately.

For infants under 1 year: DO NOT use abdominal thrusts. Use 5 back blows + 5 chest thrusts only.

High Fever in Children

A temperature above 38°C (100.4°F) in a child requires immediate action.

IMPORTANT
1

Check the Temperature

Use a thermometer. Fever = 38°C or above. Very high fever = 40°C+. Note the reading and time.

2

Give Paracetamol

Give children's paracetamol (do NOT give aspirin to children). Follow the dosage on the packaging by weight.

3

Cool & Hydrate

Remove excess clothing. Sponge with lukewarm (not cold) water. Give plenty of fluids — water, oral rehydration salts.

4

Seek Medical Help

Go to clinic immediately if: fever above 40°C, seizures, difficulty breathing, rash, stiff neck, or fever lasts 2+ days.

Malaria Alert: In Sierra Leone, any fever in a child should be tested for malaria. Go to a clinic for a rapid test.

Snake Bite First Aid

Sierra Leone has venomous snakes. Act quickly and calmly.

URGENT
1

Move Away Safely

Get the victim away from the snake immediately. Do NOT try to catch or kill the snake. Keep the victim calm and still.

2

Keep Limb Low

Keep the bitten limb below heart level. Remove watches, rings, or tight clothing near the bite. Immobilise the limb.

3

Do NOT Do These

Do NOT cut the wound. Do NOT suck out venom. Do NOT apply tourniquet. Do NOT apply ice. Do NOT give alcohol.

4

Rush to Hospital

Transport the victim to a hospital immediately. If you can, describe the snake's appearance to doctors. Antivenom may be needed.

Complete Malaria Guide

Everything you need to know about malaria — the number one killer disease in Sierra Leone.

#1 Killer Disease in Sierra Leone
~40% of hospital visits are malaria
10–15 Days until symptoms appear
100% Preventable with right steps

The Parasite

Malaria is caused by Plasmodium parasites — mainly P. falciparum in Sierra Leone, which is the most dangerous type. The parasite lives inside red blood cells.

The Carrier

Only the female Anopheles mosquito carries malaria. She bites mainly between dusk and dawn. One bite from an infected mosquito is enough to transmit malaria.

How it Spreads

Mosquito bites infected person → Parasite grows in mosquito → Mosquito bites healthy person → Parasite enters bloodstream → Attacks liver then red blood cells.

How It Does NOT Spread

Malaria does NOT spread through: touching, hugging, sharing food or water, coughing, sneezing, or casual contact. You cannot catch malaria from another person.

Days 1–3 after bite

No symptoms yet — parasite is growing silently in your liver.

Days 10–15 — Early Symptoms

High fever (38–40°C+), chills and shivering, severe headache, muscle and joint pain, fatigue and weakness, nausea and vomiting.

Severe Malaria — EMERGENCY

Confusion or unconsciousness, convulsions/seizures, difficulty breathing, inability to sit up, yellow skin/eyes (jaundice), very pale skin (severe anaemia). GO TO HOSPITAL IMMEDIATELY.

Children at highest risk: Children under 5 can develop severe malaria very quickly. Any fever in a child should be tested for malaria the same day.

Sleep Under ITN Net

Always sleep under an insecticide-treated mosquito net (ITN). This is the single most effective protection. Tuck it under the mattress and check for holes regularly.

Effectiveness: Very High

Indoor Residual Spraying

Allow health workers to spray the inside walls of your home. The spray kills mosquitoes that rest on walls after biting. Accept free government spraying programmes.

Effectiveness: High

Eliminate Standing Water

Mosquitoes breed in still water. Empty containers, buckets, tyres, blocked gutters, and flower pots regularly. Cover water storage containers with tight lids.

Effectiveness: High

Wear Protective Clothing

Wear long sleeves and trousers from dusk to dawn when mosquitoes are most active. Light-coloured clothing is better — mosquitoes are attracted to dark colours.

Effectiveness: Medium

Use Repellents

Apply insect repellent containing DEET to exposed skin. Burn mosquito coils or use plug-in repellents indoors. Neem oil is a natural alternative.

Effectiveness: Medium

Intermittent Preventive Treatment

Pregnant women should take IPTp (sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine) during antenatal visits. Children under 5 may receive SMC (seasonal malaria chemoprevention) tablets.

Effectiveness: Very High
1

Get Tested Immediately

Go to your nearest health clinic for a Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) or blood smear. Results are available in 15 minutes. Testing is free at government clinics. Never self-diagnose.

2

Take ACT Medication

If positive, you will receive Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy (ACT) — the gold standard treatment. Common ACTs in Sierra Leone: Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL), Artesunate-Amodiaquine. Treatment is FREE at government health facilities.

3

Complete the Full Course

Take ALL the medicine even if you feel better after 1–2 days. Stopping early causes drug resistance and the malaria can return stronger. Full course is usually 3 days.

4

For Severe Malaria

Severe malaria requires hospital admission and IV Artesunate injection — not tablets. If someone has seizures, loss of consciousness, or cannot take oral medicine, call 999 and rush to hospital immediately.

Important: Do NOT take chloroquine for malaria in Sierra Leone — most P. falciparum parasites here are resistant to it. Always use ACT prescribed by a health worker.

Common Disease Prevention

Prevent these common diseases with simple everyday actions.

Cholera

Waterborne

How it spreads

Contaminated water and food. Common during rainy season when floods wash sewage into water sources.

Prevention

  • Boil or treat all drinking water
  • Wash hands with soap before eating
  • Use proper latrines — no open defecation
  • Wash fruits and vegetables thoroughly

Warning Signs

Sudden severe watery diarrhoea (rice-water stool), vomiting, rapid dehydration. Can kill in hours. Go to clinic immediately.

First Response

Give Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) — mix 1 litre clean water + 6 tsp sugar + 1 tsp salt. Go to hospital urgently.

Tuberculosis (TB)

Airborne

How it spreads

TB bacteria travel through the air when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or speaks. Prolonged close contact is needed for transmission.

Prevention

  • BCG vaccination at birth
  • Ensure good ventilation in homes
  • Avoid prolonged close contact with TB patients
  • Test all household contacts when someone has TB

Warning Signs

Cough lasting 3+ weeks, coughing blood, night sweats, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, chest pain.

Treatment

TB is CURABLE. Free 6-month treatment at government clinics. Must complete ALL medication even when feeling better.

Typhoid Fever

Foodborne

How it spreads

Contaminated food and water. Spread by food handlers who haven't washed hands. Common in areas with poor sanitation.

Prevention

  • Drink only boiled or treated water
  • Wash hands thoroughly before eating/cooking
  • Eat hot, freshly cooked food
  • Avoid raw vegetables in contaminated areas

Warning Signs

Sustained high fever (39–40°C), headache, stomach pain, loss of appetite, "rose spots" on chest. Diarrhoea or constipation.

Treatment

Antibiotics (prescribed by doctor). Rest and plenty of fluids. Hospitalisation for severe cases.

Diarrhoea in Children

Preventable

Leading cause of child death

Diarrhoea kills more children under 5 than almost any other disease in Africa. Most cases are preventable.

Prevention

  • Breastfeed exclusively for 6 months
  • Wash hands with soap after toilet
  • Use clean water for preparation of food
  • Rotavirus vaccination (available free)

Immediate Treatment (ORS + Zinc)

Give ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution) at every loose stool. Give zinc tablets for 10 days. Continue breastfeeding. Seek help if blood in stool or child seems very sick.

Meningitis

Urgent

What is it

Inflammation of membranes surrounding brain and spinal cord. Can be bacterial (very dangerous) or viral. Can kill within 24 hours.

Warning Signs — CALL 999

  • Severe sudden headache
  • Stiff neck (cannot touch chin to chest)
  • High fever + sensitivity to light
  • Purple rash that doesn't fade under pressure
  • Confusion or seizures

Prevention

Meningococcal vaccine (ask at clinic). Avoid sharing cups, utensils with infected people. Good ventilation.

HIV/AIDS

Manageable

How it spreads

Unprotected sex, sharing needles, from mother to child during pregnancy/breastfeeding. NOT by hugging, sharing food, or mosquitoes.

Prevention

  • Use condoms correctly every time
  • Test and know your status (FREE and confidential)
  • Never share needles or syringes
  • Pregnant women: take PMTCT medicines

Treatment (ART)

HIV is NOT curable but is MANAGEABLE. ART (antiretroviral therapy) is FREE at government clinics. With ART, people with HIV live long, healthy lives. U = U (Undetectable = Untransmittable).

Fire & Accident First Aid

Know what to do in fire emergencies and accidents. Call 999 or 019 (Fire Service) immediately.

Burns First Aid

Caused by fire, hot liquids, steam, chemicals, or electricity. Act immediately to reduce damage.

URGENT
1st Degree

Red, painful skin (like sunburn). No blisters. Treat at home.

2nd Degree

Blisters, deep redness, wet/shiny skin. Go to clinic.

3rd Degree

White/charred skin, no pain (nerve damage). EMERGENCY — call 999.

1

Cool the Burn

Run COOL (not cold/ice) water over the burn for 20 minutes. This stops the burning process in the tissue. Do NOT use ice, butter, toothpaste, or oil.

2

Remove, Do Not Pull

Gently remove clothing and jewellery near the burn UNLESS stuck to the skin. If stuck, do NOT pull — cut around it.

3

Cover the Burn

Cover with a clean, non-fluffy material (like cling film or a clean plastic bag). Do NOT use cotton wool or anything that sticks.

4

Get Medical Help

Seek medical help for any burn larger than a postage stamp on the hand/face/genitals, any 2nd/3rd degree burn, or any burn on a child or elderly person.

Never: break blisters, apply butter/oil/toothpaste/ice, use fluffy cotton bandages. These worsen infection and tissue damage.

If a Fire Breaks Out in Your Home

Seconds matter. Know the plan before it happens.

CRITICAL
1

Alert Everyone

Shout "FIRE!" loudly to warn all people in the building. Do NOT waste time collecting belongings. Human life first.

2

Get Out Low

Stay low where air is cleaner. Feel doors before opening — if hot, do NOT open. Use stairs, never lift/elevator. Close doors behind you to slow fire spread.

3

Help Others Out

Help children, elderly, and disabled people first. If someone is trapped, do NOT re-enter — tell fire services exactly where they are.

4

Call 019 & 999

Once outside and safe, call 019 (Fire Service) and 999 (Emergency). Give exact location, how many people are involved, and whether anyone is still inside.

Fire Extinguisher

Remember PASS: Pull the pin, Aim at base, Squeeze handle, Sweep side to side. Only use on small, contained fires.

If Clothing Catches Fire

STOP — don't run. DROP — to the ground. ROLL — roll back and forth to smother flames. Cover face with hands.

Kitchen Fire

For cooking oil fire: turn off heat and cover pan with a lid or damp cloth — NEVER use water. Water on oil fire causes explosive spread.

Electrical Fire

Turn off electricity at the mains first. Do NOT use water on electrical fires. Use a CO₂ or powder extinguisher. Unplug devices if safe to do so.

Road Accident First Aid

Sierra Leone has high road accident rates. Know how to help safely.

URGENT
1

Make the Scene Safe

Turn on hazard lights. Place warning signs/rocks behind the vehicle. Do NOT move victims unless there is immediate danger (e.g., fire). Call 999 first.

2

Check Consciousness

Talk to the victim, tap gently. If unconscious but breathing, place in recovery position (on their side). If not breathing, begin CPR only if trained.

3

Control Bleeding

Apply firm pressure to wounds with any clean cloth. Do NOT remove the cloth. Do NOT remove objects embedded in wounds — leave them in place.

4

Stay & Direct Help

Stay at the scene to direct ambulance. Keep the victim still, warm, and calm. Reassure them help is coming. Note what happened for medical staff.

Neck injury: If you suspect a spinal injury (vehicle accident, fall), do NOT move the person unless in immediate danger. Wait for emergency services.
